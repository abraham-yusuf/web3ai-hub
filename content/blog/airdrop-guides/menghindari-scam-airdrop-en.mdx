## Avoiding Airdrop Scams: Complete Safety Guide

The crypto airdrop space is full of opportunities, but also full of traps. Every year, thousands of users lose assets by falling for airdrop scams. This article covers the red flags you need to recognize and safety tips to protect your wallet.

---

### 1. Never Share Your Seed Phrase

This is rule number one that must never be broken under any circumstances.

- **No legitimate project will ever ask for your seed phrase** — for airdrop claims, verification, or any reason
- **Common scam**: DMs on Discord/Telegram from someone posing as an "admin" asking for your seed phrase for "wallet verification"
- **Common scam**: Fake websites asking you to "import wallet" by entering your seed phrase
- **Even fake "customer support"** on Twitter will try to obtain your seed phrase

**If anyone asks for your seed phrase, it's 100% a scam. No exceptions.**

---

### 2. Verify Contract Addresses

Scam tokens are often sent to your wallet unsolicited, then redirect you to malicious websites.

- **Don't interact with tokens you don't recognize** that appear in your wallet
- **Check contract addresses** on Etherscan, Block Explorer, or official verification platforms
- **Use tools like Token Sniffer** or **RugCheck** to check contract reputation
- **Watch for approval requests** — scam tokens often request unlimited approval that can drain your wallet
- **Revoke approvals** periodically using Revoke.cash

**Real example:** A "USDC Airdrop" scam token appears in your wallet. When you try to swap it, a fake website requests unlimited approval that ultimately drains all your real USDC.

---

### 3. Verify Social Media Accounts

Scams often use fake accounts that closely resemble official ones.

- **Check for verification badges** — but don't rely solely on this since badges can be purchased
- **Look at follower count** and account age — scam accounts are usually newly created
- **Check bio links** — make sure they point to official domains, not lookalike domains (typosquatting)
- **Common scam domains**: `project-name.io` vs `project-name.co`, `app-uniswap.org` vs `app.uniswap.org`
- **Verify through multiple sources** — check CoinGecko, CoinMarketCap, or the project's official website

**Real example:** A fake Twitter account impersonating @zksync with the handle @zksync_airdrop directing users to a phishing website for "claiming airdrops" that actually steals private keys.

---

### 4. Watch for Phishing Websites

Phishing websites are becoming increasingly sophisticated and hard to distinguish from real ones.

- **Always type URLs manually** or use bookmarks — don't click links from DMs or emails
- **Check SSL certificates** (https://) but remember that phishing sites can also have SSL
- **Examine domains carefully** — a single character difference can mean a scam
- **Use browser extensions** like Pocket Universe or Fire that detect dangerous transactions
- **Don't connect your wallet** to websites you haven't verified beforehand

**Real example:** A website `claim-starknet[.]com` that mimics the StarkNet interface and asks users to "connect wallet" then sign a transaction that sends all tokens to the scammer.

---

### 5. "Too Good to Be True"

If something seems too good to be true, it probably is.

- **Airdrops promising thousands of dollars** without real activity are almost always scams
- **"Claim now or lose forever"** is a pressure tactic to make you panic and stop thinking clearly
- **"Send 1 ETH, receive 2 ETH"** — this is always a scam, 100% without exception
- **Tokens that suddenly appear** in your wallet with very high value are usually fake tokens
- **Airdrops that require deposits** or payments for "gas fees" to specific addresses are scams

**Common scam pattern:** A website showing "you're eligible for 50,000 TOKENS!" then asking you to connect wallet and sign a transaction that's actually transferring your tokens to the scammer.

---

### 6. Fake Team Profiles

Scams often create fake team profiles to build credibility.

- **Verify team members** on LinkedIn — watch whether profiles are real or newly created
- **Check profile photos** using reverse image search — scammers often use stock photos or other people's images
- **Look for consistency** — are team members actually active in the community?
- **Be wary of projects with no doxxed team** that promise large airdrops
- **Check GitHub** for real development activity

**Real example:** A "DeFi Protocol X" project displaying fake team photos (taken from stock photo websites) and LinkedIn profiles that were only created weeks before the "airdrop announcement."

---

### 7. Suspicious Token Approvals

Token approvals are one of the most dangerous ways scammers steal assets.

- **Don't approve unlimited spending** unless you're completely confident in the contract
- **Use tools like Revoke.cash** to monitor and revoke approvals periodically
- **Watch what permissions are requested** — if a swap asks for approval over all your tokens, that's suspicious
- **Set approval limits** to the amount you need, not unlimited
- **Read transaction details** before signing — don't just click "Confirm"

**Real example:** A user swaps 1 token on a scam website, but the transaction approval requested is unlimited access to all USDC in the wallet. The scammer then drains all USDC at any time.

---

### Red Flags Summary

Here are red flags you should always watch for:

- Requests for seed phrases or private keys
- Websites asking to "import wallet"
- DMs from "admins" or "support" on Discord/Telegram
- Unrecognized tokens appearing in your wallet
- Airdrops requiring deposits or payments
- Very urgent deadlines ("24 hours left!")
- Contracts requesting unlimited approval
- Links sent via DM rather than from official websites

---

### Additional Security Tips

- **Use a hardware wallet** (Ledger, Trezor) for large holdings
- **Enable 2FA** on all crypto-related accounts
- **Use a separate email** for crypto activities
- **Update wallet software** regularly
- **Join communities** to get scam warnings from fellow users
- **Bookmark official websites** of all projects you follow

---

### Key Takeaways

- Your seed phrase is the key to your wallet — never share it with anyone
- Verify all links, contracts, and social media accounts before interacting
- Don't panic when there's an "urgent deadline" — scammers use urgency to make you careless
- Use security tools like Revoke.cash and anti-phishing browser extensions
- If it seems too good to be true, it's probably a scam
